## Ancient Tales of Beginning

Curiosity has always been part of us since the very beginning. Many tales were written because we could not explain the mysteries of the world around us such as the origin of humans, the sun and the moon, earthquakes, rainbows, and more. We were fascinated by how the world worked, yet we lacked the means to explain it. Stories and myths became a way to satisfy our curiosity.

Let us begin with Egyptian mythology. Ra, the God of the Sun and creator of the universe, was believed to have existed eternally, uncreated and existing by himself. He decided to create his children by his own will, Shu the God of Air and Tefnut the Goddess of Moisture. Shu and Tefnut later had two children, Geb the God of the Earth and Nut the Goddess of the Sky. In the beginning, Earth and Sky were locked together in an eternal embrace. Shu, the God of Air, separated them, creating the world as we know it with the sky above and the earth below.

Traumatic experiences also inspired myths, such as the Great Flood which appears in many traditions including Aztec, Greek, Hindu, and Mesopotamian stories. These tales often describe the gods sending a flood to cleanse the world when humanity had become corrupt or disobedient.

In Greek mythology, Zeus, king of the gods, decided to flood the earth after humans became wicked. Only Deucalion and his wife Pyrrha survived, having been warned by Deucalion’s father Prometheus, a Titan. After the floodwaters receded, they sought guidance from Themis, the Titaness of divine order, law, and prophecy. She told them to throw the bones of their mother over their shoulders, a cryptic phrase meaning the stones of Mother Earth. When they did so, the stones Deucalion threw became men, and the ones Pyrrha threw became women. In this way, humanity was reborn.

Many natural phenomena once seemed mysterious because there were no scientific explanations. Today, technology and science have revealed the causes of these events. In the past, however, learning through stories was the only way people could make sense of the world.

## From Telescopes to the Big Bang

Our understanding of the world has been constantly evolving. In the past, we thought the stars above us were nothing more than flowers in the sky. Galileo Galilei changed that view when he improved the telescope to a magnification about twenty times greater than earlier versions. Through this instrument, he made groundbreaking discoveries and supported the theory of Nicolaus Copernicus, which stated that the Earth revolves around the Sun rather than the Sun revolving around the Earth. At that time, such ideas were considered dangerous. Publishing scientific works that contradicted religious doctrine could bring condemnation from the Church, and some thinkers faced imprisonment or worse.

Isaac Newton developed the theory of gravity, explaining not only why objects fall but also how celestial bodies move and how the Earth remains in orbit. His laws provided the mathematical foundation for understanding planetary motion, and many units and concepts in physics today are derived from his work. Newton’s contributions shaped the framework of modern physics itself.

In the twentieth century, Albert Einstein transformed our view of the universe. His theory of relativity showed that time is not absolute but depends on the observer’s frame of reference. This was revolutionary for a world that believed time flowed at a constant rate. When you look at the Sun, you are seeing it as it was eight minutes ago, because light takes that long to reach Earth. The stars you see at night often shine from millions of years in the past. By this point, science had begun to explore both the vast cosmos and the microscopic quantum world.

Georges Lemaître proposed the Big Bang theory, suggesting that the universe began from an extremely dense and hot singularity that expanded to form countless galaxies. As galaxies formed, so did planetary systems. In our Solar System, the creation of planets was violent and competitive. Unstable planetary embryos were destroyed or ejected, leaving only the stable survivors. The asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter is thought to be the remnant of material that never became a planet because Jupiter’s strong gravity disrupted its formation.

From the Renaissance to the Enlightenment, humanity shifted from explaining the world through myths and authority to understanding it through observation, experimentation, and reason. Stories gave way to knowledge built on evidence.

## Decades of Technological Revolution

Prepare our food and bows, and the captain stands in front of the steering wheel. We set sail, ready to explore the world. The sun shines brightly during the day, and at night countless stars please the eye. We wonder what we might find. New materials of high value, unfamiliar trees and animals, cultures and histories never seen before. Everything we discover is untold in our books. We have heard stories of dragons, phoenixes, unicorns, and griffins. These legends inspire us to search for their existence, believing they might be somewhere in the world. In the end, we encounter species far beyond our imagination. There are no old tales that speak of dinosaurs or megalodons, yet there are stories of the kraken and the leviathan. Our myths could not foresee the creatures that truly existed.

In the early decades of modern history, many revolutionary technologies were invented unexpectedly. Nineteenth-century artists painted visions of what the twenty-first century might look like. They imagined flying trains, airships shaped like giant bats, and fantastical vehicles. Instant messaging on a phone was not in their vision. Instead, they pictured postmen on flying bicycles delivering letters. They imagined the future through the lens of their present knowledge, which is why their visions seem strange to us today.

Are we born too early? Consider the devices you have used from your birth until now. If you were born before the internet, you might have used a mobile phone with physical buttons. Messages were short, and you sent them sparingly. To listen to music, the radio was useful, but you could not request a specific song. For news, you turned on the television, which also offered entertainment shows. If a movie you loved was on air and you wished to watch it repeatedly, you could buy a CD or a VHS tape.

After the internet arrived, everything changed dramatically. Almost everything moved to a screen. Sending messages, making transactions, watching entertainment, reading news, and much more became possible from one device. If you had a hobby and wanted to share it, you no longer needed television studios or recording contracts. You could simply set up your phone, record yourself, and share it instantly. The internet made life easier in countless ways.

The story did not end there. A new innovation appeared, called Artificial Intelligence. AI was created to help humanity complete tasks more efficiently and to provide information and knowledge with unprecedented speed. If you ask whether humanity has truly changed, compare ancient warfare to modern times. In the first century and even the sixteenth century, soldiers still used bows, shields, and melee weapons, with only gradual improvements. From the Second World War to the present, however, the difference is enormous. Today, drones can be used in warfare, a tool unimaginable in the 1940s. We should be grateful for the pace of change in our era.

## A Future We Might Never See

Do we actually think our vision of the future is accurate, or could it be as misguided as what 19th-century artists once imagined? New technological innovations might emerge that we cannot even begin to conceive. In the future, tools and machines beyond our imagination may exist. Our current ideas of flying cars, faster-than-light spaceships, and teleportation might look primitive or even laughable to future generations.

According to the Kardashev Scale, there are five main civilization types. Type I harnesses all the energy available on its home planet, Type II uses all the energy from its star, Type III controls the energy of its entire galaxy, Type IV taps into the energy of the universe, and Type V potentially uses energy from multiple universes. Humanity is currently estimated to be around 0.72 on this scale. It might take 100 to 200 years for us to reach Type I, and possibly thousands of years to reach Type II. However, progress could be faster or slower depending on our technological breakthroughs.

Why does it matter? Mortality still defines us. We have not yet found a cure for cancer, and natural disasters remain beyond our control. A large asteroid impact could cause mass extinction. Even with Type I status, we would only have fully mastered Earth’s resources, not eliminated all risks. We still cannot stop volcanic eruptions or tsunamis with certainty. We do not yet have vaccines for every type of cancer. The idea of modifying telomeres to extend human life is being studied, but immortality or even “amortality” is far from reality. The question remains whether humanity will ever truly overcome death itself.

Are we born too early to explore the universe? A crewed mission to Mars may happen within decades, but billions of planets remain unreachable. We cannot yet travel between them the way we move from one country to another. We lack faster-than-light travel or instant teleportation. In this sense, we are like sailors and explorers of the past such as Marco Polo and Ibn Battuta. They lived in the early days of world exploration and could not see the complete map of Earth. If they were alive today, they would be astonished to see the whole globe mapped and imaged.

Although there is still much we do not understand, we have made remarkable progress in understanding our planet, its lifeforms, and the cosmos. We know how Earth formed, we have traced the origins of our species, and we have mapped much of the observable universe. This knowledge would have been unimaginable seven centuries ago. We should embrace what we already know, continue learning from our discoveries, and appreciate the excitement of wondering what the future will look like.
