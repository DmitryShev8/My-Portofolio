## How I Got Into Programming and What I’ve Learned

My interest in technology started early. As a kid, I spent hours using a computer running Windows XP, which at the time felt like peak innovation. I even learned how to count and read with a computer.

The early 2000s were a fascinating era. It was a time when the internet was becoming more accessible, the concept of the World Wide Web was expanding rapidly, and new technologies seemed to appear every year. I didn’t fully understand everything back then, but I was curious. And curiosity tends to age well.

In school, I naturally gravitated toward mathematics. I enjoyed solving problems step by step and understanding the logic behind each solution. There’s a unique kind of satisfaction after solving a difficult problem, the kind that makes you forget how long you’ve been staring at it.

I also developed an interest in chemistry. Interestingly, it shares a similar structure with mathematics. Both require logical thinking, structured steps, and attention to detail. Looking back, those subjects quietly prepared me for programming long before I wrote my first line of code.

Programming didn’t just teach me how to write code, it changed how I think.

• It taught me how to break down complex problems into smaller, manageable parts  
• It trained me to think analytically, starting with proper identification before solving a problem  
• It taught me patience, especially when nothing works  
• It forced me to accept that errors are part of the process, not a sign of failure  

And perhaps most importantly, it taught me that debugging is just detective work, except you’re also the criminal.

## Adapting in the Era of AI

We’re currently living in one of the most transformative periods in technology, the rise of artificial intelligence.

Instead of resisting change, I chose to embrace it.

I began exploring machine learning, deep learning, and computer vision. Tools like TensorFlow and PyTorch became part of my daily workflow. Instead of using AI just to get answers, I realized that understanding how it works beneath the surface is far more important.

In this era of AI, there is no reason for me to stop learning. I always want to learn more. AI is evolving rapidly, so keeping up with these changes is no longer optional, it is essential.
