## Nihilism and the Edge of Death

Life has no objective meaning, morality is a social construct, God is not relevant, and old systems must be abolished. This is what nihilism usually says. But here is the thing. Have you ever experienced a near-death experience? If that already happened, what came to your mind? What did you say? “Thank God, He gave me a chance” or “There was a small probability that I just survived”?

One of the greatest novelists had this experience and wrote some of the most memorable books of all time until this day. His name is **Fyodor Dostoevsky** from Russia.

He used to be part of what was called the “Petrashevsky Circle.” It was an intellectual group that discussed utopian socialism, criticized the oppression of farmers, read literature censored by the government, and promoted political reform in Russia.

Tsar Nicholas I was a conservative leader who was truly against revolution. A wave of revolution happened across countries such as France, Germany, and Austria. So, he was afraid that the same tragedy would also happen in Russia. He had strict rules to avoid revolution, from executions to forced labor for those who dared to discuss socialism and openly criticize the church and the country.

Dostoevsky was accused of reading and sharing a letter criticizing the Orthodox Church, of participating in discussions questioning the stability of the country, and of having sympathies for revolutionary ideas. On 22 December 1849, Dostoevsky and many members of the group were brought to Semyonovsky Field. The process was strict. They were sentenced to death. Some were prepared to be hanged, and others to be shot. But Tsar Nicholas I decided to grant them “salvation.” The sentence was changed to forced labor in Siberia for four years, followed by military service. Many historians believe this was planned all along to give them psychological terror.

## The Moment Before Death

This experience had a big impact on his psychology. He wrote in a letter that, while waiting for execution, he realized that life is truly meaningful; even the last minute feels priceless. He felt a deepened love for life. Even the sun's light looked so beautiful.

This existential trauma became one of the psychological foundations in his novels such as *Crime and Punishment*, *The Idiot*, and *The Brothers Karamazov*.

> “The darker the night, the brighter the stars, the deeper the grief, the closer is God! Pain and suffering are always inevitable for a large intelligence and a deep heart. The really great men must, I think, have great sadness on earth.”

He was interested in progressive ideas and rational thought in an era when Russians started to choose nihilism. But the near-death experience gave him a realization that the meaning of life doesn’t come from theory, but from extreme existential experience. If life really has no meaning, then that last moment should mean nothing as well.

---

## Suffering and Faith

While he was in Siberia, he lived with killers, robbers, and violent inmates. He found something shocking: people who suffered had the strongest faith. He believed that suffering is not the proof of the absence of meaning. On the contrary, through suffering, humans find their deepest soul. So this is when he started to fully reject the idea of nihilism.

Let me tell you one example from his book *Crime and Punishment*. Raskolnikov said in nihilistic logic:

“If God doesn’t exist, morality is relative, then killing is permissible for a big idea.”

But the result only gave emptiness, inner torture, and alienation. Dostoevsky concluded that nihilism sounds intellectually clever, but it destroys people spiritually.

## Reflection

Nihilism is one of the most followed ideas until this day, especially among atheists. I don’t give any argument against nihilism. But I have some reflection questions for you to answer.

First, it is about morality. I partly agree that morality is relative. For example, we used to think that there was nothing wrong with slavery as long as the master didn’t harm the slave. But now we agree that the practice of slavery is no longer permissible because it is against free will. But let us rethink it relative to the ontology (technically, more radical). This is what Dostoevsky was afraid of.

“There is no standard of morality at all. Good and evil are only social constructs.”

If morality is really a social construct, then decisions could change if we have power in government. This is where the danger is found. If there is no absolute moral standard, then the things left are greed, power, extreme utilitarian logic, or individual will. Do you really believe that torturing children can be permissible based on a certain culture, or that genocide may be permissible if the majority agrees?

The second one is about the meaning of life. Imagine a kid was born with many diseases and then dies young. If there is no such thing as God’s plan and no cosmic justice, then is the suffering itself purely absurd, or can it still have meaning? If there is no meaning in suffering, is life itself worth living?
